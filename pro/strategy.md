## 1. Strategy Blocks — full doc template (Janakiraman, Lenny's 2025; headline style traces to Lafley & Martin, *Playing to Win*)

Funnel discipline first: **50-150 raw inputs → cluster to 10-15 → ~3 pillars + explicit non-goals → ~3 HMW → 4-dim bet rubric → 5-phase timeline.** Keep small-s (this quarter) and big-S (multi-year) in ONE doc feeding ONE roadmap — never two disconnected plans. Cite as a named framework, not settled canon.

```

"We will [win X for whom] by [the one unfair thing], so that [outcome] by [when]."
   ✗ topic: "Improve onboarding"   ✓ assertion: "Become the fastest path to first
   invoice for solo founders — live in <1 day vs the category's 1 week."

## Pillars (~3 — each a where-to-play/how-to-win bet, written as a hypothesis)
For each: "We believe [action] will result in [outcome], as measured by [metric]."
  P1 ____  · DHM: Delight ___ / Hard-to-copy ___ / Margin ___ (Biddle)
  P2 ____  · DHM: ___
  P3 ____  · DHM: ___
(If a pillar has no Hard-to-copy leg, it's a feature, not a pillar — cut or merge it.)

## Non-goals — "What we're NOT doing this quarter (and why)"  [MANDATORY]
  - NOT ___  because ___      (name excluded segments + the cheaper alt you considered)
  - NOT ___  because ___

## How-Might-We (~3 — the open questions the pillars must answer)
  HMW1 ____   HMW2 ____   HMW3 ____

## Bet rubric — score each pillar 1-5 on 4 dims (uniqueness is the one teams omit)
| Pillar | Customer value | Business value | Feasibility/cost | Uniqueness/defensibility | Σ |
|--------|:--:|:--:|:--:|:--:|:--:|

commodity race — fund only if it's table-stakes parity, never as a growth bet.

## 5-phase rollout timeline (phases, not hard dates)
  Phase 1 Foundation → 2 Prove → 3 Scale → 4 Expand → 5 Defend
  Each phase: entry condition · the one metric that graduates it · kill criteria.
```

## 3. Portfolio allocation — Cooper Strategic Buckets (Cooper, *Stage-Gate*/JPIM)

Above the backlog you **allocate**, you don't rank (Perri). Set %-capacity **before** scoring, rank only *within* each bucket, and ring-fence the small-bet bucket from the urgent-core default. [bucket %s directional]

| Bucket | Illustrative capacity | Rank within by | Discipline |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core** (defend/optimize today's product) | ~60% | Productivity Index | the urgent default — cap it, don't let it eat the rest |
| **Growth** (adjacent expansion) | ~30% | Productivity Index | must clear must-meet gates |
| **New bets** (new market/tech, Type-1) | ~10% | strategic fit + option value | ring-fenced — starve-proof, or it never ships |

**Productivity Index (Cooper) = NPV ÷ constraining resource (go-forward costs only — sunk costs excluded).** Rank within a bucket by PI, not by gut. NewProd-style concept scoring runs ~73-84% pre-Development accuracy (directional). "Stage-Gate" is a trademark.

## 4. RICE-inversion — WORKED example (where naive RICE breaks on interdependence)

RICE = Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort (Intercom). It assumes items are **independent**. Marketplace backlog, 5 items; **C is shared identity/payments infra that D and E depend on.** [all numbers illustrative]

| Item | Depends on | Reach (k/qtr) | Impact | Conf | Effort (pm) | **RICE** | Naive rank |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A Seller onboarding | — | 40 | 1.0 | .80 | 4 | **8.0** | 4 |
| B Search ranking | — | 80 | 1.0 | .70 | 5 | **11.2** | 3 |
| C Identity/payments infra | — | 10 | 0.5 | .60 | 8 | **0.4** | **5 (last)** |
| D Instant payout | **C** | 30 | 2.0 | .70 | 3 | **14.0** | 2 |
| E Trust badges | **C** | 50 | 1.0 | .60 | 2 | **15.0** | 1 |

**The break:** naive RICE says build **E → D → B → A → C**. But E and D are *blocked on C* — that order is physically impossible, and the enabler everything depends on ranks DEAD LAST because its own direct reach is small and its effort large. RICE's Effort-in-denominator quietly punishes the big enabling bet; its independence assumption ignores the dependency edge entirely.

**Fix (two moves):**
1. **Respect the DAG as a hard constraint** — topologically sort, score only the *ready* set. C is the sole ready prerequisite → it goes first regardless of its score.
2. **Re-score the enabler with WSJF/CD3** (SAFe/Reinertsen), where **Opportunity-Enablement** is an explicit CoD term. CoD = User-Business Value + Time-Criticality + Risk-Reduction/Opportunity-Enablement; WSJF = CoD ÷ Duration. C inherits the value of unblocking D+E (illustrative OE=20 → CoD 33 ÷ dur 8 = **4.1**, lifting it above A=3.5 and B=3.2 instead of dead last). Then sequence: **C first**, then highest-WSJF unblocked item.

Rule: the moment items are interdependent, strategic, or reach/effort are mostly noise → stop ranking with RICE, score with cost-of-delay and sequence on the dependency graph.

## 5. Build–Partner–Buy — market-entry scoring matrix

For a new capability needed to enter a market/segment. Score each option 1-5 per criterion, weight to your context, pick the max. [weights illustrative]

| Criterion (weight) | **Build** | **Partner** | **Buy/Acquire** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Control / ownership (×3) | 5 max control | 2 low control | 4 |
| Speed to market (×3) | 1 slow | 5 fast | 4 fast |
| Cost / capital (×2) | 3 | 4 cheap upfront | 1 costly + integration risk |
| Strategic fit — is it CORE/differentiating? (×3) | 5 | 2 | 3 |
| Defensibility (DHM Hard-to-copy) (×2) | 5 owned moat | 1 rented | 4 if it adds a real moat |
| Integration / execution risk (×2) | 3 | 4 | 1 high |

**Decision heuristics (from the rubric, not just the sum):**
- **Build** when the capability is **core/differentiating** and control > speed — you're buying a moat, accept the slow clock.
- **Partner** when speed > ownership and the capability is **non-core** — fastest, lowest capital, but you're renting; never rent your differentiator.
- **Buy/Acquire** when **time-to-market is decisive AND a credible target exists** — fast + control, but price in integration risk as the #1 failure mode.

Gate the winner with the §Strategy entry sequence: bottom-up TAM/SAM/SOM → Moore beachhead test → require **≥3× Zone-of-Benefit** (the Udezues) → first-90-day milestones + explicit **kill criteria** + ring-fenced resources so the core doesn't starve the bet.

**Partner-selection cascade:**

**Once you choose 'Partner' — *select* the partner, don't just decide to.** The matrix above picks the *mode*; it's silent on *which* partner. For technology-push / open-innovation entry (Caetano & Amaral 2013, *Technology and Investment* 4(1) — extending Phaal's T-Plan, which says find partners but not *how*), cascade partner identification down the same three roadmap layers: **Market partners** (distribute/sell into the target segment, bring market intel) · **Technology partners** (co-develop the capability) · **Financial partners** (fund the tech project / provide enabling resources). Prioritise candidates on: confidence/trust · non-competing goals · capacity to pay · prior-collaboration experience · innovation expertise · reputation/honesty · motivation & interest · **cultural compatibility** (the integration-risk killer the weighted sum hides). Running tech development and product/service development at *different* partners in parallel compresses technology-transfer time — but every added partner is an integration-risk multiplier; gate each with explicit kill criteria. **MODERATE** (single research-group source; B2B / tech-push / open-innovation contexts).

## 6. Platform strategy — product-family / shared-platform decisions (Meyer & Lehnerd; Robertson & Ulrich)

**Use when** one core (parts / code / process / model) must serve several variants, editions, or segments — hardware families, multi-edition SaaS, configurable/modular products, a shared model behind many surfaces. Platform = *intentional* sharing across variants to deliver variety on a smaller cost base. The governing law (Robertson & Ulrich): every platform trades **commonality** (cost, speed) against **distinctiveness** (fit to each segment); the architecture sets that trade. Run the 8 IN ORDER — it's a teaching synthesis (MIT, *Designing Product Families*, Simpson & de Weck), not a single canonical "named framework," so cite the underlying canon, not the course.

1. **Strategic intent** — cost-savings (leverage one core across variants) OR new-markets/revenue (reach segments you couldn't serve one-off)? Name it; the two imply different sharing (Robertson & Ulrich's two motives; Meyer & Lehnerd "platform leveraging").
2. **Define market segments** — the **market segmentation grid** (Meyer & Lehnerd): 2 axes (e.g. price/performance tier × segment or job), 4–25 cells. (Pairs with Aumayr segmentation, §Strategy.)
3. **Commonality impact on the operator** — does the customer benefit from shared parts/code, or is the commonality "hidden" (benefits only the firm)? Will a user own one variant or many? (Robertson & Ulrich: is the shared element on a *differentiating* attribute the customer perceives?)
4. **Expected customization** — bundled (features ladder up by tier) vs mix-and-match (build-your-own). Express every difference in the LANGUAGE OF THE CUSTOMER'S NEED, not internal part names (§Discovery: need = direction+metric+goal).
5. **Cost structure** — where do savings land: non-recurring (shared development/testing) vs recurring (economies of scale, learning curve, bulk purchasing)? Is the line **dev-cost-dominated** (software → commonality pays in shared build) or **manufacturing/COGS-dominated** (hardware → commonality pays in scale)? (Meyer & Lehnerd platform economics.)
6. **Market-variant plan + volumes** — place each variant on the segment grid with a planned volume %.
7. **Planning horizon + architectural stability** — how many years can you confidently plan? Split the STABLE core (slow refresh — plan long) from FAST-refresh modules (electronics/AI/UI — short clock); share against the stable core, vary the fast bits (Ulrich modularity / carry-over).
8. **Sharing strategy** — at WHAT hierarchy level is sharing happening (whole product / major assembly / module / component / line-of-code), and which modules are **common / configurable / unique**?

*Sources: Marc Meyer & Alvin Lehnerd, The Power of Product Platforms (1997) — market segmentation grid, platform leveraging; David Robertson & Karl Ulrich, "Planning for Product Platforms," Sloan Management Review 39(4), 1998 — commonality vs distinctiveness; synthesized for teaching in MIT Professional Education's Designing Product Families (Simpson & de Weck).*

## 7. Commonality vs distinctiveness — divergence & the management-lever menu

**Sources:** levers — Cameron, Crawley, Kwapisz & McNeill, *Management Levers for Product Platforms*, EMJ 29(3) 2017 (12 industrial case studies); divergence & lifecycle offsets — Boas, Cameron & Crawley (*Systems Engineering* 2013; Boas, MIT diss. 2008); commonality-metric lineage — de Weck (*not* a co-author of the lever paper). Research is rooted in complex-HARDWARE families (aircraft, autos, satellites, capital equipment) — see **Translate to software** at the end. All specific levers/numbers below are illustrative unless sourced.

**The core failure — divergence:** a product family realizes LESS commonality than planned because customization/optimization fights cost control, and commonality *erodes over time* unless governed — it is NOT self-sustaining. The first/lead variant pays the shared-platform investment while later variants reap the benefit, so scoring only variant 1's ROI makes the platform look unjustified; the savings live downstream.

**6 divergence drivers — the last 3 are the manager-controllable ones:** market change · learning (the planned premium turns out too costly) · new tech supersedes old interfaces · **lifecycle offsets** (serial design → mature variants resist change, so changes propagate only to earlier-stage variants; *partly* controllable via sequencing) · **failure to weigh lifecycle benefits** (near-term horizon; scoring variant 1 alone) · **intentional pursuit of uniqueness** (building unique when a capable common part already exists).

**Two penalties to price in:** *commonality risk* — a shared part that fails has a broad blast radius across the whole family; *capability penalty* — a common part specced to the most-demanding variant over-serves and overcharges the low-end variant.

**Management levers — pick a mix to match the imbalance (control = set the rules; incentive = shape behavior); specific levers illustrative:**

| Type | Control | Incentive |
|---|---|---|
| **Financial** | scope-of-investment eval (count ALL variants, not just #1); dev/production-cost allocation; mandatory co-investment | new-part fee (illustrative ~$7k/part); tax non-common parts; subsidy pool for common parts; transfer pricing |
| **Technical** | commonality metric (common ÷ total parts); tag intended-common parts; architecture control (fixed interfaces); variant-exclusion matrix | part-proliferation studies; shared reuse database; platform education |
| **Organizational** | a commonality OWNER who sees BOTH cost and savings; module-governance board; cross-functional design reviews | commonality target in review/comp; lead-development project; order variants by volume |

**Highest-leverage lever (MODERATE — synthesized from 12 hardware cases, not a law):** a single **commonality owner** with visibility to both the upfront investment and the downstream savings. Absent that, every variant optimizes locally and the platform diverges by default.

**Translate to software:** shared services, component libraries, design systems, and monorepos diverge the same way. "First variant pays the bill" = the first team funds the platform tax later teams free-ride on; *commonality risk* = a shared-service/dependency failure with a wide blast radius; *capability penalty* = an over-general shared abstraction that bloats the simplest consumer. The governing move is identical — name an owner who sees both sides of the ledger, and weigh the WHOLE family's lifecycle, not the first team's quarter.

## 8. Design for Variety — isolate what will change (Martin & Ishii, *Design for Variety*, Res Eng Design 13, 2002)

A peer-reviewed NPD method for deciding **what to standardize vs what to modularize** across a product family. Two indices, both built from *specification flows* (the design info one component must pass to another):
- **Generational Variety Index (GVI)** — estimated redesign effort a component needs to meet FUTURE requirements (external drivers: changing needs, regs, competitor moves, cost-downs). HIGH-GVI = will change a lot across generations.
- **Coupling Index (CI)** — strength of those spec flows. **CI-S (supplying)** = specs this part feeds others, so changing it forces THEM to change (propagation). **CI-R (receiving)** = specs this part takes from others, so it must change when THEY do (vulnerability).

The method ranks components by GVI and CI and concentrates redesign where it buys the most future flexibility — "where to standardize, where to modularize" (Martin & Ishii). Operationalized as a decision rule:
- **Standardize** the high-redesign-cost component you want to carry across generations: invest to drive its GVI *and* its CI-R toward zero so neither future requirements nor neighbors' changes can force it to move — it becomes the durable platform core.
- **Modularize / decouple** the high-CI-S *and* high-GVI parts: put the volatile, high-propagation pieces behind a STABLE INTERFACE so they can vary without cascading into the core.

Reusable heuristic: **predict what must vary (GVI), then isolate it behind a fixed interface (kill the coupling)** so variety is cheap and the core is durable. A CI-R-vs-GVI plot (bubble size = CI-S) is a handy way to rank where to focus. *(Software translation: the fast-changing surface — prompts, models, ranking, UI — sits behind a stable contract; the core domain model stays put.)*

Confidence: **HIGH** on the indices and attribution. The crisp standardize-vs-modularize decision rule and the CI-R/GVI plot are this skill's operationalization of the paper's GVI+CI analysis — useful, but not verbatim Martin & Ishii. Blind spot: GVI is an *estimate* of future requirements; misjudge what varies and you'll standardize the wrong core.

## 9. Multi-sided platform pricing — which side to subsidize (Eisenmann, Parker & Van Alstyne, HBR 2006; Parker & Van Alstyne, Mgmt Sci 2005)

A platform prices *per side*, not per product. One side is the **subsidy side** (priced at/below cost — sometimes free — to build the volume the other side wants); the other is the **money side** (pays a premium for access to that volume). The subsidy buys **cross-side network effects**: a large subsidy-side base makes money-side users pay handsomely to reach it. Article examples: Adobe Acrobat Reader is free (~500M readers) while document producers pay (~$299) for that audience; game consoles subsidize gamers (hardware at/below cost) and charge developers a per-title royalty.

**Four factors decide which side to subsidize (HBR 2006):**
1. **Price sensitivity** — subsidize the more price-sensitive side; charge the side that values access to the other more.
2. **Quality sensitivity (counterintuitive)** — *charge* the side that **supplies** quality and *subsidize* the side that **demands** it; the charge acts as a quality filter. Verbatim: "rather than charging the side that strongly demands quality, you charge the side that supplies quality." Consoles: subsidize gamers (demand quality), levy a developer royalty (supply quality) so weak titles don't clear the bar.
3. **Same-side network effects** — seed density where a side attracts its own kind; where a side repels its own kind (sellers fear rivals), consider granting one marquee user category-exclusivity and charging rent for it — then police it so the exclusive doesn't gouge the other side.
4. **Output cost** — a giveaway is safe only when each subsidy-side user costs ~$0 (a digital good). With real unit cost, if money-side WTP fails to materialize the giveaway bleeds — prove money-side WTP before subsidizing a physical good.

**Marquee/anchor-user lever:** secure the exclusive participation of a side-defining user (anchor tenant, flagship developer, large buyer) to pull the other side onto the platform.

**Appropriability caution (multi-homing — Rochet–Tirole / Eisenmann's later platform work, *not* the HBR pricing factors):** a subsidy is wasted if the side you subsidize can reach a *rival's* money side instead of yours; when the money side is contestable (low switching / cheap multi-homing), you fund the category and a competitor captures the rent. Confirm your money side is appropriable before subsidizing the other. (Netscape's free browser couldn't anchor server sales once rival servers reached the same users — illustrative, not from HBR 2006.)

**Contrast with single-product pricing (base §Pricing / PRO pricing):** EVC / Van Westendorp / Gabor-Granger optimize one product's price; platform pricing optimizes the *system*, so that machinery applies *within* a side, not across the platform. (Widely-cited illustration: Microsoft subsidized developers with cheap/free tooling while Apple's Mac tooling was pricier and more closed, and Windows accreted the larger app ecosystem — illustrative platform history; specific dollar/multiple figures are not well-sourced, so don't cite them.)

## 10. Winner-take-all & the fight-vs-share decision (Eisenmann/Parker/Van Alstyne, HBR 2006)

A two-step call before a platform land-grab.
**Step 1 — will this market be served by a SINGLE platform?** Yes iff all three hold:
- **High multi-homing cost on ≥1 side** — running a 2nd platform is expensive (one OS per PC). Low multi-homing → platforms coexist (credit cards: most merchants take all of them).
- **Strong positive cross-side network effects** (same-side effects reinforce only where they're positive; they're often negative, e.g. seller-vs-seller) — users converge on the biggest network.
- **No strong preference for special features** — if a segment has unique needs, a differentiated niche survives in the leader's shadow. Amex keeps fat margins at ~5% of Visa's card count (*directional*) via no-preset-spending-limit — a feature Visa structurally can't match.

All three → the industry tips to one platform (DVD makers pooled tech into a single 1995 format rather than fight).
**Step 2 — fight for proprietary control, or share the platform?** A bet-the-company call.
- **Fight** only when you can plausibly win — needs ≥1 of: a cost/differentiation edge · preexisting user relationships (Adobe leveraged its PostScript installed base to launch Acrobat/PDF) · a reputation for past prowess · deep pockets for a war of attrition.
- **Share** when you might *not* win: sharing *expands total market size and lessens rivalry and marketing outlays* (the article's words) — and sidesteps a format-war standoff (the VHS-vs-Betamax trap DVD makers chose to avoid).

**Get-big-fast caveat:** racing to amass users is often right but risky when (a) the business isn't readily scalable (needs skilled humans per transaction) or (b) funding could dry up mid-burn if capital-market sentiment turns. And **first-mover advantage is real but not decisive: when a market evolves *slowly*, late-mover advantages can be more salient** (more time to learn from the pioneer's mistakes).

## 11. Platform envelopment & openness (Eisenmann/Parker/Van Alstyne, 'Platform Envelopment,' SMJ 2011 + HBR 2006; Gawer, Research Policy 2014)

**Envelopment — the threat DHM moat-decomposition misses.** You can nail pricing, win winner-take-all, and still be killed by an *adjacent* platform that shares your user base and **bundles your function into its own at no extra price**. Microsoft bundled streaming into Windows/NT Server and enveloped RealNetworks; mobile phones swallowed cameras, music players, PCs, wallets. The standalone can't respond — it can neither cut its money-side price nor assemble a comparable bundle. Three defenses (Eisenmann's HBR 2006 'threat of envelopment'): **switch your money side / change the model** (Real ceded free streaming, launched Rhapsody — now *charges* the consumers it used to subsidize); **find a 'bigger brother'** (ally with a platform that can counter-attack — Real + cable/cellular carriers); **sue** (platform antitrust is unsettled — Real extracted a **$761M settlement** from Microsoft in 2005; Sun and Time Warner/Netscape similarly reaped bounties). Vigilance is structural: when boundaries blur, attacks come from any direction — but a focused firm can beat a big diversified one precisely because envelopment needs cross-business-unit cooperation incumbents fumble (Sony had the pieces, couldn't coordinate them).

**Openness is the governance lever (Gawer integrative framework).** Both meanings of 'platform' create value through **economies of scope**: the economics view (two-sided market) = scope in **demand** (cross-side network effects); the engineering view (modular **core + periphery** architecture) = scope in **supply/innovation** (re-use of components and complements). The reframe for PMs: **a complementor is an innovator/supplier, not a consumer** — so how open your interfaces/APIs are sets their incentive to build on you. Openness rises along a continuum: **internal** (closed) → **supply-chain** (interfaces shared with suppliers) → **industry/ecosystem** (open APIs; an unlimited pool of outside innovators who *self-identify* — Apple 'walled garden' vs more-open Android). More open = more external innovation — but an **inverted-U**: past a point, opening access *crowds out* complementor incentive as competition compresses their margin (Boudreau 2010, *Mgmt Science*). And openness invites **role-shift** — a complementor that grows can turn competitor (Netscape→Microsoft; Facebook Home on Android). Tune openness to maximize ecosystem innovation you can still *govern and capture*; it's the multi-sided analog of build-vs-partner-buy. [Boudreau inverted-U: MODERATE — single empirical stream.]

## 12. Chicken-and-egg / cold-start — igniting critical mass (Caillaud & Jullien 2003; Eisenmann/Parker/Van Alstyne 2006)

Steady-state value is min(supply, demand) (base §Calibration; this module routes liquidity depth to pro §metrics §1). The **cold-start** problem is a *different* problem — neither side joins before the other exists, so there is no liquidity to instrument yet. You must manufacture **critical mass** on the side the other needs most, then let the virtuous cycle take over. Five ignition tactics:
- **Subsidize one side (the subsidy side).** Charge little/nothing to — even pay — the *more price-sensitive* side and/or the side whose presence most attracts the other, and recover margin on the side with higher WTP for access (Eisenmann/Parker/Van Alstyne's **subsidy side vs money side**). Choosing the subsidy side *is* the first cold-start decision.
- **Standalone single-player utility.** Give one side a reason to show up with *zero* users on the other (a tool useful solo: restaurant-management software before any diners). "Come for the tool, stay for the network."
- **Marquee / anchor users.** Land a side-defining participant whose presence pulls the other side; pay for *exclusivity* when a few large users are pivotal and you want to deny them to rivals (Eisenmann et al.).
- **Seed / hand-build supply.** Concierge or manually recruit one side until organic supply takes over — the end-to-end concierge / Wizard-of-Oz pattern (base §Discovery; pro §discovery).
- **Narrow the launch market.** Ignite in one city / category / campus where critical mass is *reachable*, then expand; local liquidity beats global thinness.

Pick the side, pick one ignition tactic, and **instrument per-segment liquidity from Wave 0** (base §Calibration). Once a segment is liquid, switch to the steady-state liquidity + two-North-Star instrumentation (pro §metrics §1).

## 13. Fast-start roadmapping workshops (S-Plan / T-Plan; Phaal/Farrukh/Probert, IfM Cambridge)

When you need alignment fast, the *process is the deliverable* — roadmaps are "dirty mirrors" (a Philips exec, via Phaal): the exercise reflects back the hidden gaps in current strategy, good and bad. Treat the problems a roadmap surfaces as the *point*, not a flaw. Run it as a workshop, not a doc:
- **Customise first — no single format fits** (Phaal/Farrukh/Probert, *Customizing Roadmapping*, RTM 2004). Design layers/sub-layers, timeframes and process to the question *before* the session; the canvas IS the facilitation tool. Start simple (paper/whiteboard), add software only once the process is stable.
- **S-Plan (strategic landscape; Phaal et al. 2007):** one big wall-chart across the whole topic to build the 'landscape' and spot 'landmarks' (opportunities/issues), then small groups deep-dive priority topics on a common template (~1½ days). Good for the fuzzy front end.
- **T-Plan (fast-start, ~1–2 days; Phaal et al. 2001):** four linked workshops — **market → product → technology → charting** — connected by **linkage grids** (a grid mapping how each layer's items drive the next; see §6 platform decisions for the analogous share-vs-vary mapping). Adds analytical structure as the innovation matures.
- **Think big, act small/fast:** prototype the method on one topic, scale as confidence grows.
- **Guard the consensus.** Cross-boundary communication/consensus is roadmapping's most-cited benefit — so deliberately fight groupthink: Motorola required a **'minority report'** on every consensus roadmap. Pair with a written-first pre-mortem (§Discovery).
- **Stay strategic — avoid too much detail:** navigational tool, not a project plan.
- **Living artifact:** refresh on the *system's* rate of change (software fast, infra slow) + triggers (competitor move, regulation); occasionally redraw blank-sheet.

Staff three roles: **owner/customer** (owns content, budget, decisions), **facilitator** (runs the process), **functional experts** (own the layers). Success = confidence + consensus on the decisions arising each iteration, **not** forecast accuracy. **HIGH.**
